Hierarchical Protein Structure: Primary, Secondary,...

  • Secondary Structure: Local Folding Patterns
    • Highly regular local structures stabilized by hydrogen bonds between the protein backbone's amide and carbonyl groups.
    • Alpha-Helices: Coiled structure, stabilized by H-bonds between every fourth amino acid.
    • Beta-Sheets: Extended structure formed by lateral H-bonds between polypeptide chains. Can be parallel or antiparallel.
    • Loops/Turns: Connecting regions between helices and sheets.
    • Visual: Show diagrams of alpha-helices and beta-sheets, highlighting hydrogen bonds. Optionally include a Ramachandran plot to show allowed phi/psi angles.

Forces Driving Protein Folding: Stability in Numbers

Forces Driving Protein Folding: Stability in Numbers

  • Hydrogen Bonds:
    • Individually weak (2-5 kcal/mol) but collectively strong.
    • Form between backbone atoms and/or side chain atoms.
    • Highly directional; crucial for stabilizing secondary structures (α-helices, β-sheets).
    • Visual Suggestion: Show a diagram of hydrogen bonds within an alpha helix and between beta strands.

Forces Driving Protein Folding: Stability in Numbers...

  • Disulfide Bridges:
    • Covalent bonds (strong: ~60 kcal/mol) between cysteine residues.
    • Stabilize tertiary and quaternary structures.
    • Common in extracellular proteins (e.g., antibodies, secreted enzymes).
    • Visual Suggestion: Illustrate a disulfide bond formation between two cysteine side chains.

Forces Driving Protein Folding: Stability in Numbers...

  • Hydrophobic Interactions:
    • Major driving force for protein folding: "hydrophobic collapse".
    • Hydrophobic amino acids (e.g., Val, Leu, Ile, Phe) cluster in the protein's interior, away from water.
    • Increases entropy of surrounding water molecules, contributing to overall stability.
    • Visual Suggestion: Show a protein with hydrophobic residues clustered in the core.

Forces Driving Protein Folding: Stability in Numbers...

Anfinsen's Experiment: The Sequence Holds the Key

  • Ribonuclease (RNase) Refolding:
    • Denatured RNase (unfolded, inactive) spontaneously refolds into its active, native conformation.
    • Conditions: Removal of denaturant and oxidizing agents.
  • The Sequence Holds the Key:
    • Anfinsen's experiment demonstrated that the amino acid sequence contains all the information needed for a protein to fold correctly.
    • The protein "self-assembles" to its functional 3D structure.
  • Thermodynamic Drive:
    • Protein folding is driven by thermodynamics.
    • The native state represents the most thermodynamically stable (lowest energy) conformation.
    • The protein searches the conformational space until it finds the global minimum.

Thermodynamics of Folding: The Energy Landscape

  • Proteins Seek Stability: Proteins fold to achieve their lowest energy conformation, known as the native state. This conformation is crucial for biological function.
  • The Energy Landscape Funnel:
    • Visualize protein folding as a journey down an "energy funnel."

    • As the protein folds, it descends the funnel toward lower energy states.
    • Narrow bottom: Represents the native state (low entropy, low energy, high stability).
