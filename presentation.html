Forces Driving Protein Folding: Stability and Thermodynamics

Forces Driving Protein Folding: Stability and Thermodynamics

  • Driving Forces: Several forces drive protein folding:
    • Hydrophobic Interactions: Major force! Nonpolar amino acids cluster in the protein's interior to minimize contact with water.
    • Hydrogen Bonds: Form between backbone atoms and side chains, stabilizing secondary and tertiary structures.
    • Disulfide Bridges: Covalent bonds between cysteine residues, contributing to stability (especially in extracellular proteins).
  • Hydrophobic Effect:
    • Core principle: Increase in entropy of water molecules when nonpolar groups are sequestered from the aqueous environment.
    • Hydrophobic collapse: Initial stage of folding, driven by this effect.

The Protein Folding Problem: Levinthal's Paradox

The Protein Folding Problem: Levinthal's Paradox

  • The Paradox: Predicting protein structure by randomly searching all possible conformations is computationally impossible.
    • Proteins can explore an astronomical number of conformations.
    • Even for a small protein (e.g., 100 amino acids), the number of possible conformations is estimated to be 3<sup>198</sup>.
    • Searching these conformations at nanosecond speed would take longer than the age of the universe!
  • Observed Reality: Proteins fold rapidly and efficiently in vivo.
    • Folding often occurs within seconds or minutes.
    • This suggests a directed folding pathway, not a random search.
35

The Protein Folding Problem: Levinthal's Paradox (Part 2)

  • Implications:
    • Protein folding is not a trial-and-error process.
    • Proteins must follow a specific, energetically favorable path.
    • This implies the existence of folding mechanisms and guiding forces that reduce the conformational search space.
    • Understanding these forces is critical for predicting protein structure.

Anfinsen's Experiment: The Power of Primary Structure

  • Anfinsen's Revelation: Primary structure dictates 3D shape.
  • The Experiment: Christian Anfinsen's Nobel Prize-winning work with Ribonuclease A.
    • Denaturation: Ribonuclease A unfolded using urea and beta-mercaptoethanol (to break disulfide bonds).
    • Refolding: Removal of denaturants allowed the protein to spontaneously refold.
    • Result: Full enzymatic activity regained! The protein returned to its native conformation.
