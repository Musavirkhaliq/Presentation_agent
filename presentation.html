* Monomers that polymerize to form polypeptide chains (proteins)
  • * The Basic Structure:
* Central carbon (alpha carbon) bonded to: * Amino group (-NH₂) * Carboxyl group (-COOH) * Hydrogen atom (-H) * Side chain (R group) - the variable part that defines each amino acid
  • * 20 Common Amino Acids:
* Classified based on the properties of their R-groups: * Hydrophobic: Tend to cluster together; important for protein folding and stability. Example:* Alanine, Valine, Leucine, Isoleucine, Phenylalanine * Hydrophilic: Interact favorably with water.   • * Side Chain Dictates Properties
* Size, shape, charge, hydrogen-bonding capacity, reactivity determine the role of an amino acid in a protein. * Hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, disulfide bonds are all dependent on these properties.

Hierarchical Protein Structure: Primary and Secondary

Hierarchical Protein Structure: Primary & Secondary

  • * Primary (1°) Structure: The Blueprint
* Linear sequence of amino acids linked by peptide bonds. * Dictated by the gene sequence. * Determines all subsequent levels of structure. * Analogy: Like letters forming a word. Visual Suggestion: Show a short amino acid sequence, highlight the peptide bonds.*
  • * Secondary (2°) Structure: Local Folding
* Local, repeating patterns of folding in the polypeptide chain. * Primarily stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms (C=O and N-H). * Main types: * α-Helices: Coiled structure with H-bonds along the helix axis. Example: Keratin. * β-Sheets: Extended strands arranged side-by-side, H-bonds between strands. Can be parallel or anti-parallel. Example: Fibroin (silk). * Loops/Turns: Connect α-helices and β-sheets; often found on the protein surface. Visual Suggestion: Show 3D models of alpha helix and beta sheet structures with hydrogen bonds highlighted.*
  • * The Protein Backbone: Foundation for Folding
* Repeating sequence of N-Cα-C atoms in the polypeptide chain. * Side chains extend outward from the backbone. Visual Suggestion: A simplified diagram of the polypeptide backbone, labeling the N, Cα, C atoms.*
  • * Backbone Torsion Angles: Phi (Φ), Psi (Ψ), and Omega (ω)
* Describe the rotation around the bonds in the polypeptide backbone. * Phi (Φ): Rotation around the N-Cα bond. * Psi (Ψ): Rotation around the Cα-C bond. * Omega (ω): Rotation around the peptide bond (C-N), usually 180° (trans conformation). Visual Suggestion: A diagram showing the polypeptide backbone with Phi, Psi, and Omega angles clearly labeled.*
  • * Ramachandran Plot: Allowed Conformations
* A plot of Phi (Φ) vs. Psi (Ψ) angles. * Shows the sterically allowed conformations for a polypeptide backbone. * Different regions of the plot correspond to different secondary structures (α-helix, β-sheet). Visual Suggestion: A Ramachandran plot showing the regions corresponding to alpha-helices, beta-sheets, and other conformations. Overlay a few data points from a real protein.*

Forces Driving Protein Folding

Forces Driving Protein Folding

  • * Hydrogen Bonds:
* Backbone H-bonds stabilize secondary structures (α-helices & β-sheets). * Side-chain H-bonds contribute to tertiary structure stability. Visual:* Show a diagram of hydrogen bonds in an alpha helix and between side chains in a folded protein.
  • * Disulfide Bridges:
* Covalent bonds between cysteine residues (-S-S-). * Important for stabilizing tertiary and quaternary structures. * Common in extracellular proteins. Visual:* Illustrate disulfide bond formation between two cysteine residues.
  • * Hydrophobic Packing:
* Hydrophobic amino acids cluster in the protein core, away from water. * Major driving force for folding. * Increases entropy of water molecules. Visual:* Depict a protein with hydrophobic residues (e.g., leucine, valine) clustered in the interior.
  • * Electrostatic Interactions:
* Attraction and repulsion between charged residues * Important for stabilizing tertiary structure. Visual:* Show positive and negative charges interacting between residues.
  • * Forces Act in Concert:
* No single force acts in isolation. * Together, these interactions determine the unique and stable 3D structure of a protein. Visual:* A schematic showing all forces acting on a single folded protein. Illustrate how these are all interconnected.

The Protein Folding Problem: Levinthal's Paradox

  • * Levinthal's Paradox: The Folding Conundrum
Proteins can't possibly sample all* conformations to find their native (functional) state. Imagine a protein with 100 amino acids, each with just 3 possible conformations. That's 3100 (or roughly 5 x 1047) possibilities! * Even at incredibly fast sampling rates, it would take far longer than the age of the universe to try every conformation. This contradicts the fact that proteins fold spontaneously in seconds or minutes. * The implication: Proteins don't fold randomly!
  • * Specific Folding Pathways
* Proteins fold via defined pathways, guided by their amino acid sequence. * These pathways involve: * Formation of local secondary structures (alpha-helices, beta-sheets). * Hydrophobic collapse (burying hydrophobic amino acids in the protein's core). * The amino acid sequence encodes the information needed to guide the protein along a specific folding route.
  • * Visual Suggestion:
* A diagram contrasting random conformational sampling vs. a funnel-like energy landscape illustrating guided folding pathways. Show a protein going down the funnel to find the correct folding pathway. * A simple calculation demonstrating the immense number of possible conformations (e.g., with the 3100 example) and contrasting that time frame with how long it actually takes for a protein to fold. * Show a visual representation of Anfinsen's experiment to reiterate that the amino acid sequence contains all the information needed to specify 3D structure.

Anfinsen's Experiment: Sequence Determines Structure

  • * Central Dogma in Action:
* Anfinsen proved sequence dictates structure.
  • * The Experiment:
* Ribonuclease (enzyme) denatured (unfolded) using urea and reducing agent. Upon removal of denaturants, ribonuclease spontaneously* refolded to its native, active state.
  • * Key Finding:
* The amino acid sequence contains all the information needed to determine the protein's 3D structure and function.
  • * Implications:
* Proteins, in theory, can fold correctly on their own. * Revolutionized our understanding of protein folding
  • * Important Note:
Many proteins require assistance from chaperone proteins for proper folding in vivo*.

Thermodynamics of Protein Folding: Energy Landscapes

  • * Protein Folding: A Thermodynamic Imperative
* Spontaneous process driven by thermodynamics. Proteins seek the lowest free energy state. * Analogy: Think of a ball rolling downhill – it's going to find the lowest point.
  • * Energy Landscapes: A Visual Metaphor
* Represent the energy of a protein as a function of its conformation. * Each point on the landscape represents a different possible 3D structure. * The height of the landscape represents the energy of that conformation.
  • * The Funnel Model: Guiding the Fold
* A folding funnel depicts the overall trend of decreasing energy as the protein folds. * Proteins don't explore all possible conformations randomly (Levinthal's Paradox solution!). * They are guided down the funnel towards lower energy states. Visual:* A cartoon of a folding funnel, showing multiple pathways converging to a single native state at the bottom.
  • * Native State: The Energy Minimum
* The native state is the most thermodynamically stable conformation. * Represents the global minimum on the energy landscape. * Essential for proper function. Visual:* Highlight the bottom of the funnel in the previous cartoon, labeling it "Native State".
  • * Implications
* Understanding energy landscapes helps us understand misfolding and aggregation diseases.
