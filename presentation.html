Peptide Bonds and Polypeptide Formation

  • Peptide Bond Formation
    • Amino acids are linked via peptide bonds during translation.
    • Dehydration Reaction: Carboxyl group (-COOH) of one amino acid reacts with the amino group (-NH2) of another. A water molecule (H2O) is released.
    • Forms a covalent bond between the C and N atoms.
  • Polypeptide Chain: Primary Structure
    • The linear sequence of amino acids is the primary structure of a protein.
    • N-terminus (amino end) to C-terminus (carboxyl end).

Levels of Protein Structure: Primary

  • Primary Structure: The Blueprint
    • The linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain.
    • Determined by the genetic code (mRNA sequence).
    • Analogous to the order of letters in a word - specific sequence dictates function.
    • Crucially: Dictates all higher levels of protein structure (secondary, tertiary, quaternary).
    • Think of it as the foundation upon which the protein's 3D architecture is built. Altering the primary structure can have significant consequences on protein function.

Levels of Protein Structure: Quaternary

Quaternary Structure: Multiple Subunits Unite!

  • Definition: The arrangement of multiple polypeptide chains (subunits) into a functional protein complex. Think of it as assembling individual protein "pieces" into a larger, working machine.
  • Stabilization: Subunits are held together by non-covalent interactions (hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds, van der Waals forces) - just like tertiary structure!

Levels of Protein Structure: Quaternary (Part 2)

    • Increased Stability: Complex more resistant to denaturation.
    • Regulation/Allostery: Interactions between subunits can fine-tune protein activity.
    • Efficiency: Can bring multiple active sites together for faster reaction rates.

The Protein Folding Problem and Levinthal's Paradox

  • Levinthal's Paradox: The Folding Speed Mystery
    • Proteins fold into their native 3D structure incredibly fast (milliseconds to seconds).
    • A random search of all possible conformations would take longer than the age of the universe! (Estimated at 10^(30) years for a 100 amino acid protein)
    • Visual Suggestion: A graphic illustrating the vastness of conformational space, contrasted with the speed of protein folding.
  • The Complexity of the Problem
    • Highlights the inherent challenge of predicting protein structure from sequence alone.
    • Suggests the existence of highly guided and efficient folding pathways.

Anfinsen's Experiment: Sequence Determines Structure

Anfinsen's Experiment: Sequence Determines Structure

  • Central Question: Can a denatured protein spontaneously refold into its native, active conformation?
  • The Experiment: Christian Anfinsen studied ribonuclease A (RNAse A), a small enzyme with four disulfide bonds.

32

Anfinsen's Experiment: Sequence Determines Structure...

  • Denaturation:
    • Used urea to disrupt non-covalent interactions.
    • Used Î²-mercaptoethanol to reduce disulfide bonds (-S-S- to -SH HS-).
    • RNAse A lost all enzymatic activity and unfolded.
  • Refolding:
    • Removed urea and Î²-mercaptoethanol slowly.
    • RNAse A spontaneously refolded to its native, active state.
    • Catalytic activity was almost fully restored.
