Defining Artificial Intelligence

Slide 2

  • * What is AI?
* Systems designed to perform tasks that typically require human intelligence. * Mimics cognitive functions like learning and problem-solving. * Enables machines to perceive, reason, and understand language.
  • * Key Capabilities:
Learning: Acquiring information and rules for later use. Example: Image recognition gets better with more images.* Reasoning: Applying logic to arrive at conclusions. * Automation follows pre-programmed instructions. AI learns and adapts to new situations. It's not just about doing a task, but improving over time!*

* Funding cuts due to unmet expectations and computational limitations. * Struggles with complex, real-world problems (e.g., natural language processing). * Two major "AI Winters" in the 1970s and 1980s. * Visual Suggestion: A graph depicting AI funding over time, highlighting the "winter" periods.
  • * Resurgence & Advancements
* New approaches (e.g., expert systems, machine learning) led to renewed interest. * Increased computing power and availability of data fueled advancements. * Deep Learning revolutionizes fields like computer vision and natural language processing. * Visual Suggestion: Images showcasing advancements in AI like image recognition or examples of Large Language Models.

Slide 4
!two person standing on gray tile paving Photo by Ian Schneider on Unsplash

Narrow AI (Weak AI) vs. General AI (Strong AI)


    
  
  • *   Narrow AI (Weak AI):
    • 


    *   Designed and trained for specific tasks.
    *   Excels at its defined purpose but lacks general intelligence.
    *   Examples:
        *   Voice assistants (Siri, Alexa)
        *   Spam filters
        *   Product recommendation systems
        *   Self-driving cars (Level 4 and below - specific operational design domain)

    
  
  • *   General AI (Strong AI):
    • 


    *   Possesses human-level cognitive abilities across a wide range of domains.
    *   Can understand, learn, adapt, and implement knowledge like a human.
    *   Hypothetical – does not currently exist.

    
  
  • *   Current State:
    • 


    *   AI today is primarily Narrow AI.
    *   Rapid advancements are constantly pushing the boundaries.

    
  
  • *   Achieving General AI:
    • 


    *   Significant challenges remain in replicating human-level consciousness, understanding, and adaptability.
    *   Its feasibility is still debated.

Machine Learning and Deep Learning

  • * Machine Learning (ML):
* AI systems that learn from data without explicit programming. * Algorithms identify patterns and make predictions. * Example: Spam filters learn to identify unwanted emails based on marked examples. * Visual: A simple graphic illustrating data flowing into a "Machine Learning Algorithm" box, resulting in "Predictions."
  • * Deep Learning (DL):
* A subset of ML using deep neural networks. * Neural networks are inspired by the structure of the human brain. * Excels at complex tasks involving images, text, and sound. * Example: Image recognition systems accurately identifying objects in photos. * Visual: A simplified diagram of a deep neural network with multiple layers.
  • * How ML/DL Improve Over Time:
* Data is Key: More data leads to better performance. * Feedback Loops: Continuous learning from successes and failures. * Enables AI systems to adapt and refine their abilities. * Visual: An icon representing data and an arrow looping back to a brain icon, representing learning.
  • * Applications:
* ML: Recommendation systems (Netflix, Amazon), fraud detection, medical diagnosis. * DL: Self-driving cars, natural language processing (chatbots, translation), facial recognition. * Impact: Revolutionizing industries through automation and enhanced decision-making. * Visual: A collage of icons representing the listed applications (e.g., a car for self-driving, a speech bubble for NLP).

Ethical Considerations and Challenges

Slide 7
!person in blue crew neck t-shirt Photo by Seiya Maeda on Unsplash

Ethical Considerations and Challenges

  • * Privacy:
* Concern: Collection and use of personal data without informed consent or adequate security. * Example: Facial recognition technology and mass surveillance. * Visual: An icon representing a data lock or a silhouette with data flowing around it.
  • * Bias:
* Concern: AI algorithms reflecting and amplifying existing societal biases. * Impact: Unfair or discriminatory outcomes in areas like loan applications, hiring processes, and criminal justice. * Example: Image recognition systems struggling to accurately identify individuals with darker skin tones. * Visual: An image showing skewed scales of justice or diverse faces with differing AI recognition results.
  • * Accountability & Transparency:
* Challenge: Difficulty determining responsibility when AI systems make errors or cause harm. * Need: Explainable AI (XAI) to understand how AI decisions are made. * Example: Self-driving car accidents and determining liability. * Visual: A "black box" representing AI with question marks around it, morphing into a partially transparent box with gears visible.
  • * Job Displacement:
* Potential: Automation leading to significant job losses across various industries. * Impact: Requires workforce retraining and adaptation strategies. * Data Point: McKinsey Global Institute estimates that automation could displace millions of workers by 2030. * Visual: A graph depicting job displacement trends or a robot replacing a human worker in a traditional job.
  • * Ethical Guidelines & Regulations:
* Necessity: Development and implementation of clear ethical principles and legal frameworks for AI development and deployment. * Goal: To ensure AI benefits society while mitigating potential risks. * Example: The EU AI Act aiming to regulate AI based on risk levels. * Visual: A gavel striking down next to a circuit board, symbolizing regulation of technology.
