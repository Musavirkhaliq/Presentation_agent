Protein Backbone Conformation: Torsion Angles and the...

  • Backbone Conformation & Torsion Angles
    • Protein backbone defined by repeating N-Cα-C units.
    • Phi (ϕ): Rotation around the N-Cα bond.
    • Psi (ψ): Rotation around the Cα-C bond.
    • Omega (ω): Rotation around the C-N bond (usually 180° due to partial double-bond character).
  • Steric Constraints
    • Not all ϕ/ψ angle combinations are possible due to steric hindrance.
    • Bulky side chains limit the allowable conformations.

Protein Backbone Conformation: Torsion Angles and the...

  • The Ramachandran Plot
    • A plot of allowed ϕ and ψ angles for amino acid residues in a protein.
    • Illustrates sterically allowed regions (favored conformations).
    • Different regions correspond to different secondary structures (e.g., α-helix, β-sheet).
    • Glycine has the fewest steric constraints and occupies a larger area of the plot.

The Protein Folding Problem: A Grand Challenge

  • The Protein Folding Problem: A Grand Challenge
  • Levinthal's Paradox:
    • Proteins must fold into their native 3D structure to function.
    • Enormous number of possible conformations.
    • Levinthal estimated it would take longer than the age of the universe for a protein to randomly sample all possible conformations.
    • Visual: Illustration comparing the vast number of potential conformations to the single native state.

The Protein Folding Problem: A Grand Challenge (Part 3)

  • Anfinsen's Experiment (1950s): Sequence Determines Structure
    • Ribonuclease A unfolds and loses activity in denaturing conditions (urea).

The Protein Folding Problem: A Grand Challenge (Part 4)

  • Forces Driving Protein Folding:
    • Hydrophobic Effect: Primary driving force. Nonpolar side chains cluster in the protein's interior to avoid water.
    • Hydrogen Bonds: Stabilize secondary structures (α-helices and β-sheets) and specific interactions.
    • Van der Waals Forces: Contribute to the tight packing of atoms in the protein core.
    • Disulfide Bonds: Covalent links between cysteine residues that can stabilize the folded structure (especially in secreted proteins).

Thermodynamics and the Energy Landscape

  • Protein Folding: A Thermodynamic Imperative
    • Folding is driven by the tendency to achieve a state of minimal Gibbs Free Energy (ΔG < 0).
    • The native state represents the most thermodynamically stable conformation.
  • The Energy Landscape: A Funnel Model
    • Concept: Visualizes the folding process as a journey down a funnel.
    • Funnel Shape: Represents decreasing free energy as the protein folds towards the native state.
    • Roughness: The landscape isn't perfectly smooth; small "bumps" represent local energy minima (metastable intermediates).
    • Visual: Show a diagram of the energy landscape funnel, labeling the unfolded state (high energy, many conformations) and the native state (low energy, single conformation) at the bottom.

Thermodynamics and the Energy Landscape (Part 2)

  • Minimizing Free Energy
    • Hydrophobic effect: Key driving force, sequestering hydrophobic residues inside the protein.
    • Hydrogen bonds: Stabilize secondary structures (α-helices and β-sheets) and tertiary structure.
    • Van der Waals forces: Contribute to the overall stability through close packing of atoms.
    • Conformational Entropy: Unfolded state has high entropy, folding decreases entropy, but favorable enthalpy changes overcome this entropic penalty.
    • Visual: show how hydrophobic amino acids aggregate in the interior, and polar/charged amino acids reside on the exterior of the protein molecule.
